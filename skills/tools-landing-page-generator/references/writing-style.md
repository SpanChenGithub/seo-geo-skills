# Landing Page Writing Style Guide

## 1. Purpose and Scope

Use this guide as the baseline for every landing page. Keep the copy clear, consistent, SEO friendly, GEO friendly, and useful to the reader.

Use this priority order when requirements conflict:

1. Factual accuracy and effective SEO
2. Natural, clear expression
3. This writing style guide
4. The page structure guide
5. Suggested word lengths

Required section order and item counts remain mandatory. Suggested word lengths do not justify awkward wording, hype, repetition, unsupported claims, or damage to SEO. When the exact target keyword is unusually long, exceed a suggested word length if necessary to use it naturally and accurately. Record every such exception and its reason in the QA report.

When a structure example sounds salesy, keep its required information but rewrite it in the neutral voice defined here.

## 2. Voice and Tone

- Write in American English. Follow American spelling, punctuation, and usage.
- Sound conversational, friendly, and direct. Write as if you are speaking to a friend.
- Address the reader as `you`. Use `I`, `you`, `we`, `our`, or the product name when natural.
- Mention the brand name only when it adds clarity or value.
- Use simple words and short sentences. Keep the reading level at or below seventh grade.
- Avoid empty, vague, or salesy claims. Be specific, useful, helpful, and honest.
- Suggest rather than hype. Use conditional language such as `recommend`, `might`, and `could` when certainty is not supported.

Acceptable:

> This might be a good choice if you want a free trial.

> If you want to create a cleaner result, you can use the preview before downloading.

Not acceptable:

> You will find it is amazing as soon as you start using it.

> All your problems will be solved after using this tool.

## 3. Structure and Flow

- Match the search intent. Base the copy on the verified product background, researched user profile, use cases, and high value topics.
- Use an inverted pyramid. Put the key value in the first two or three lines, then add details and proof.
- Keep paragraphs to one through four short lines. Avoid walls of text, but do not make the whole page a series of disconnected one sentence paragraphs.
- Keep sentences short and make the page flow naturally from one section to the next.
- Prefer active voice.
- Avoid repetition. State each point once and make every section add something new.
- Do not use dash characters as punctuation in user visible copy. Rewrite with a comma, a colon, or separate sentences.

The dash rule applies to user visible prose and headings. It does not apply to HTML, CSS, JavaScript, filenames, file paths, attribute names, or URL slugs where a hyphen is required by technical syntax or convention.

## 4. Section and Heading Rules

- Make section titles clear, specific, and focused on a useful benefit.
- Make the value of a section understandable as soon as the reader sees its title.
- Under each title, do one of these jobs well:
  - Explain what the tool does.
  - Explain a real user problem and how the tool helps.
  - Explain a key feature and its practical benefit.
- Avoid overlap between sections.
- Do not use unsupported superlatives such as `best`, `leading`, `ultimate`, or `number one`.
- When the page structure requires a heading with the brand and target keyword, use a neutral form such as `Why Use [Brand] for [Target Keyword]`. Do not use `Best Choice` merely because it appeared in a template.

## 5. Clarity, Substance, and Evidence

- Remove fluff. Every sentence must earn its place.
- Apply the `So what?` test. Delete a line if it does not help the reader understand, decide, or act.
- Prefer concrete details over vague claims.
- Use user supplied facts, official product documentation, or reliable public evidence for factual claims.
- Treat claims about features, pricing, free access, speed, accuracy, ratings, limits, compatibility, registration, privacy, security, storage, deletion, and third party sharing as evidence gated.
- Never borrow a competitor feature or promise and present it as a fact about the target product.
- If a required fact is unavailable, ask the user. Do not invent it. Do not leave internal evidence markers or unsupported placeholders in the final HTML.

## 6. SEO and Keywords

- Use the target keyword, high frequency terms, and semantically related terms only when they fit naturally and help the reader.
- Do not stuff keywords or repeat awkward variants for search engines.
- Let search intent and readability determine wording before keyword density.

## 7. Mechanics and Consistency

- Keep grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting consistent across the page.
- Choose one convention for a recurring style decision and use it throughout. For example, choose `%` or `percent`, then stay consistent.
- Measure word length against the final rendered English copy, not HTML tags, code comments, or accessibility attributes.
- Treat word lengths as the lowest priority requirement. Document any exception caused by a long target keyword in the QA report.

## 8. Prepublish Checklist

- The core value is clear in the first two or three lines.
- The copy matches search intent and verified product facts.
- Paragraphs are short, sentences are direct, and the flow is easy to follow.
- The tone is conversational, friendly, honest, and not salesy.
- The page addresses the reader as `you` where natural.
- Each section adds unique value and avoids repetition.
- Active voice and simple words keep the reading level at or below seventh grade.
- No dash punctuation appears in user visible copy.
- Keywords appear naturally without stuffing.
- Claims with factual, comparative, privacy, pricing, or performance implications have evidence.
- Grammar, punctuation, formatting, and recurring style choices are consistent.
