Use this reference to choose the article format and produce the complete outline. Treat the outline as a required deliverable, not as private scratch work.

## Let the SERP determine the content model

Research the current organic results for the target query before selecting a format, depth, or reference word count. Record the language, country or search locale, device context when relevant, and research date. Do not infer a country from an English-language keyword. Ask for the intended locale when it could materially change the result; if it remains unspecified, record the actual neutral or default search environment used and avoid locale-specific assumptions.

Inspect the ranking pages themselves, not titles or snippets alone. Use enough relevant organic results to identify a stable pattern, normally the first 10 distinct results when accessible. Separate editorial articles from product pages, category pages, videos, forums, and other result types. SERP modules such as People Also Ask can reveal questions, but they do not count as ranking article formats.

For each comparable editorial result, record:

- Rank, title, URL, publisher, and publication or update date when visible
- Page and article format
- Dominant search intent and likely audience
- Main-body word count or a documented estimate that excludes navigation, comments, and boilerplate
- Scope, heading pattern, comparison criteria, steps, or item count
- Evidence style, firsthand material, examples, and media
- Material strengths, omissions, and repeated claims that require verification

Use the evidence in this order:

1. Select the primary format from the dominant format among relevant results that satisfy the same intent.
2. Select depth from the decisions, questions, steps, entities, and objections the SERP shows users need resolved.
3. Calculate a descriptive reference word range from comparable pages in that primary format.
4. Use the product or editorial goal only as a tie-breaker or documented adjustment, not as a reason to ignore a clear intent pattern.

When the SERP is mixed, name one primary format and any supporting format. Explain the split instead of assembling every observed section into a hybrid. Do not copy competitors' headings, phrasing, opinions, or errors.

## Choose the primary format

| Format | Choose when the SERP and intent emphasize | Required editorial logic | Common failure to avoid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Explainer | Understanding a concept, process, issue, or decision | Define the subject early; explain why it matters, how it works, examples, limits, and next actions | Expanding a definition without helping the reader make or do anything |
| How-to | Completing a task in a sequence | State prerequisites and outcome; present ordered, testable steps; cover checks, exceptions, and troubleshooting | Calling unordered tips a tutorial or inventing product steps |
| Listicle | Scanning multiple tips, ideas, examples, methods, or resources | State the list's organizing principle; make every item distinct and substantively useful | Repeating the same idea under different labels |
| Roundup | Choosing among several products, services, experts, examples, or solutions | Publish inclusion criteria and methodology; evaluate every entry consistently; match recommendations to use cases | Ranking items without evidence or disguising sponsored placement |
| Single Review | Evaluating one named product, service, book, place, or other entity | Disclose test basis; cover intended user, setup, capabilities, limits, pricing or access when verified, pros, cons, and verdict | Presenting researched marketing copy as hands-on testing |
| Comparison | Deciding between two or more named options | Define common criteria; compare like with like; show meaningful differences, tradeoffs, and use-case recommendations | Declaring a universal winner from uneven evidence |
| Alternatives | Finding replacements for a named option | Explain switching reasons; define selection criteria; evaluate plausible substitutes and migration tradeoffs | Listing adjacent products that do not solve the same job |

Use a hybrid only when the SERP genuinely combines intents. For example, a comparison may need a short explainer before the decision matrix, and a how-to may need a concise troubleshooting list. Keep one format dominant so the promise and structure remain clear.

## Set depth and a reference word range

Calculate the reference range from at least five relevant, complete, comparable articles when possible. Report the observed median and central range, identify excluded outliers, and state any limitations in the count. Do not compare a 300-word product page with a long editorial review merely because both rank for the query.

Turn that evidence into two planning values:

- **SERP reference range:** the observed central range for comparable ranking content
- **Proposed range:** the expected length of the planned outline after accounting for missing topics, original evidence, and the chosen format

Treat both as editorial estimates. Cover the intent completely and stop when the article is useful. Do not add introductions, FAQs, examples, or repeated summaries merely to reach a count. Do not shorten necessary evidence to mimic a thin result. Document why the proposed range differs materially from the SERP range.

Depth means answer completeness, not length. Map depth to concrete reader needs such as prerequisites, steps, criteria, examples, limitations, alternatives, risks, and next actions.

## Use optional narrative frameworks carefully

Treat PAS and PAES as optional framing devices, never mandatory article structures.

- Use PAS only when a real problem, proportionate explanation of its impact, and a supported solution improve the opening.
- Use PAES as Problem, Agitate, Expertise or Experience, and Solution only when the evidence supports all four beats. The Expertise or Experience beat must come from a real author, test, research action, source, or supplied record; never manufacture it.
- When PAES is selected or requested, add an opening map to the outline: `P` names the specific reader problem, `A` shows a proportionate practical consequence, `E` identifies the traceable expertise or experience that earns the recommendation, and `S` gives the direct answer, decision path, or next action. Do not label these beats in the published prose.
- Skip either framework when the reader wants an immediate definition, instruction, comparison, or answer.
- Do not manufacture fear, urgency, pain, or personal experience to fill a framework.

Keep the opening brief. State the direct answer or article promise before extended context.

## Build the complete outline

Present the outline in the article's requested language. Include all of the following even when the final article will be short.

### 1. Strategy header

Record:

- Seed keyword and primary query
- Target language, locale, audience, knowledge level, and reader job
- Dominant and secondary intent
- Business or editorial goal and one primary next action
- Chosen primary format, any supporting format, and evidence-based rationale
- SERP reference range, proposed range, and adjustment rationale
- Working angle and working H1
- Author or creator identity when known, real expertise or experience inputs, editorial actions actually performed, and the professional point of view the article will express
- One working slug and one meta-description direction for approval
- One-sentence article promise
- Known product or subject constraints
- Research status, evidence gaps, and facts still awaiting confirmation

A working H1, slug, and meta-description direction are allowed at outline stage. Do not present multiple metadata candidates or label a title, meta description, or slug as final before delivery.

### 2. SERP and information-gain summary

Summarize:

- Dominant formats and depth patterns
- Recurring questions, criteria, steps, entities, and objections
- Useful PAA or related-query evidence when available and relevant
- Common gaps, weak explanations, stale claims, or missing audience needs
- The article's planned information gain
- Firsthand inputs available, requested, unavailable, or not applicable
- Experience plan: the sections that will use documented firsthand observations, the record supporting each one, and the sections that will rely on research-based professional judgment instead

Information gain must be specific. Examples include an original test, expert interview, internal data, worked example, decision framework, verified product detail, clearer calculation, regional nuance, or synthesis that changes the reader's decision. Rewording several competitors is not information gain.

### 3. Section map

Use a table with one row per H2 and important H3:

| ID | Proposed heading | Reader question or job | Direct answer or section thesis | Supporting points | Evidence or source IDs | Original value or firsthand input | Media | Internal link | CTA or transition | Est. words |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Make the outline executable. Replace vague entries such as "discuss benefits" with the actual benefits, limits, examples, and proof to cover. Show heading hierarchy, but allow natural wording changes during drafting.

For every material section:

- State the answer, conclusion, or job of the section
- List the necessary facts, examples, steps, or comparison criteria
- Map evidence to claims and mark unsupported items as `needs confirmation`
- Identify the information gain or state that the section is supporting context
- Identify any firsthand input without implying it exists when it does not
- Map every planned experiential statement to a real author, use session, test, interview, output, screenshot, note, or log
- Estimate section length from the information required
- Note media, internal links, and conversion transitions only when useful

### 4. Opening and ending plans

For the opening, specify the direct answer or promise, context the reader genuinely needs, and whether an optional narrative framework will be used. If PAES applies, record the four-beat opening map and the evidence behind `E`. Establish authority with a specific judgment, useful distinction, or observed pattern. Do not write a long generic hook, research-process caveat, non-testing disclaimer, guarantee disclaimer, or publisher-placement defense.

For the ending, specify the decision, action, checklist, or concise synthesis the reader should leave with. Avoid repeating the entire article. Add a CTA only when it fits the user's goal.

### 5. Evidence and decision requests

End the outline with:

- Claims that require primary-source verification
- Product facts, access, samples, or expert input requested from the user
- Planned disclosures, including affiliate, sponsorship, testing, or AI-assisted-media disclosures when applicable. Keep them concise, factual, and naturally placed in a byline, author note, methodology, or relevant claim. Do not plan a bold `Disclosure:` block by default, and do not pair a relationship statement with a defensive paragraph about work that was not performed.
- Material editorial choices requiring user confirmation

## Apply format-specific requirements

Add the relevant fields below to the section map.

### Explainer

Include a plain-language definition, significance, mechanism, example, limits or misconceptions, and practical next step. Put the definition near the start.

### How-to

Include the outcome, prerequisites, required tools or inputs, ordered steps, verification after important steps, common failures, troubleshooting, and the finished state. Use only verified workflows. If a critical step is unknown, stop and request it.

### Listicle

Define the list's scope and organizing rule. Give each item a distinct takeaway, evidence or example, when to use it, and any meaningful caveat. Use a comparison table only when the same fields apply to most items.

### Roundup

State the selection universe, inclusion and exclusion criteria, evaluation method, evidence date, and conflicts or commercial relationships. Express a publisher or author relationship briefly and confidently, then move to the criteria and judgments; never disguise sponsored placement or turn the opening into a legalistic disclaimer. Describe work performed rather than missing test inputs. Organize entries by a reader-centered principle instead of explaining that the publisher's product appears first. Use consistent fields across entries and plan recommendations by use case rather than relying on an unexplained ordinal ranking.

### Single Review

State whether evidence comes from direct testing, a supplied test record, a demo, documentation, third-party research, or a combination. Plan sections for setup, core use, results, limitations, pricing or access when verified, support, privacy or ownership when material, pros, cons, ideal user, poor-fit user, and verdict. Never label research as hands-on experience.

### Comparison

Define shared criteria before evaluating options. Plan a fact-checked summary table, criterion-by-criterion analysis, meaningful tradeoffs, switching costs, and recommendations for distinct use cases. Keep unknown cells visible as `not confirmed` rather than guessing.

### Alternatives

Explain why users seek a replacement, then map each alternative to those switching criteria. Include relevant compatibility, migration, learning curve, pricing or access, feature gaps, and best-fit scenario when verified. Do not include an option solely to increase the item count.

## Plan conditional sections

Add a section only when it resolves a real intent or decision:

| Section | Add when | Do not add when |
| --- | --- | --- |
| FAQ | Distinct high-value questions remain after the main structure | It would repeat headings or exist only for keywords or schema |
| PAA answers | Current PAA evidence is available, relevant, and answerable | Questions are unrelated, redundant, or unsupported |
| Troubleshooting | Users can encounter identifiable failure states | The workflow or fixes are unverified |
| Glossary | Several necessary terms would otherwise interrupt the article | Only one or two terms need ordinary inline definitions |
| Comparison table | Options share meaningful, verified criteria | Cells would be vague, uneven, or mostly unknown |
| Methodology | Selection, ranking, testing, or calculations affect trust | The article makes no evaluative or empirical claim |
| Further reading | Real related resources help the reader continue | Destinations are invented, weak, or purely promotional |

FAQ and PAA content are not quotas. Resolve a question in the main body when that is the clearest place, and do not repeat it in an FAQ.

## Plan media and links

Create a preliminary media plan inside the outline:

| Asset ID | Placement | Purpose | Asset type | Source or creation method | Evidence vs illustration | Rights or permission | Alt-text intent | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Use media to demonstrate a step, result, distinction, dataset, example, or decision. Do not prescribe an image every fixed number of words. Mark illustrative or AI-generated visuals clearly; never use them as evidence of product behavior or real-world results.

Create a preliminary internal-link map with the exact known destination URL, proposed descriptive anchor, placement, and reader benefit. If no real URL is available, mark the item as a recommendation and keep it out of publishable article links.

## Enforce outline approval

End with a clearly labeled approval request that summarizes the chosen format, proposed depth, reference word range, information-gain plan, and unresolved decisions.

Do not draft the full article until the user explicitly approves the complete outline. Silence, approval of the general topic, prior approval of research, or a request to "continue" before the outline is shown does not count as outline approval. Revise and resubmit the outline when requested.

If an approved outline later needs a material change to its primary format, search intent, conclusion, comparison set, evidence basis, or scope, pause and obtain approval for the revised outline before continuing. Minor heading wording and paragraph-level edits do not require renewed outline approval.
