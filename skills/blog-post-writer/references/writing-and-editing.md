Use this reference only after the complete outline has received explicit approval. Draft from verified evidence, add genuine information gain, and edit for a direct, natural reading experience.

## Honor the approved outline

Use the approved strategy, format, scope, conclusion, and evidence plan as the drafting contract. Improve phrasing and paragraph flow freely, but pause when a new finding would materially change the article's intent, primary format, comparison set, thesis, or evidence basis. Revise the outline and obtain renewed approval before making that change.

Do not treat the proposed word range as a quota. Expand a section only to answer a real question, show evidence, clarify a decision, or add a useful example. Remove filler even if the result is shorter than planned.

## Build an evidence and claims ledger

Maintain a working ledger for material claims:

| Claim ID | Draft claim | Claim type | Source or firsthand record | Date checked | Confidence | Allowed wording | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Classify claims as:

- **Verified primary fact:** supported by official documentation, original data, a direct record, or the subject itself
- **Verified secondary fact:** supported by a credible independent source
- **Firsthand observation:** directly observed through a documented test, interview, visit, or use session
- **Attributed opinion:** clearly assigned to its speaker or source
- **Inference:** supported reasoning that must be labeled and not overstated
- **Unknown:** request, omit, or mark internally as unresolved

Give extra scrutiny to claims about pricing, availability, performance, security, privacy, legal rights, health, finance, specifications, integrations, limits, market leadership, ratings, and superiority. Verify time-sensitive facts near delivery. Do not turn a competitor's statement or a SERP snippet into verified subject truth.

Use calibrated wording. Replace an unsupported absolute with a precise supported statement, not with vague promotional language.

## Add information gain without inventing experience

Make the article contribute something beyond a paraphrase of ranking pages. Useful information gain may come from:

- A documented original test with inputs, environment, method, date, and observed output
- A real author, user, or expert interview with permission and attributable notes
- Internal data whose sample, timeframe, method, and limits can be disclosed
- A worked example, calculation, template, checklist, or decision tree created from verified facts
- A clearer synthesis that resolves contradictions or maps tradeoffs to different use cases
- Current primary-source details that competing coverage omits or gets wrong
- Product-specific constraints, edge cases, or implementation advice supplied by the user

Never claim to have used, tested, bought, visited, interviewed, measured, or witnessed something unless the underlying record exists and is available for review. Do not invent quotes, people, credentials, screenshots, test data, dates, device details, sample sizes, or outcomes.

If firsthand experience is unavailable:

1. Build authority from work actually performed: current source verification, workflow inspection, comparison, synthesis, examples, and decision frameworks.
2. Put a concise evidence basis in the methodology or author note when it affects trust. Do not open with a defensive paragraph about tests that were not performed.
3. Avoid sensory or experiential language such as "in our testing," "we used," or "we found" when no matching record exists.
4. Ask the user for a test record, expert input, or product evidence when it is material.
5. Make confident recommendations by documented fit and explain the reasons. State only limitations that change the decision, close to the affected claim.
6. Remove the section or narrow the conclusion when the available evidence cannot support useful judgment.

Label illustrative examples, hypothetical scenarios, and generated sample outputs. Do not present them as actual results.

## Write with earned authority

Professional authority comes from specificity and sound judgment, not from repeatedly narrating research constraints.

- Lead with an answer, recommendation, decision rule, or pattern the author can support.
- Explain why a feature, tradeoff, failure mode, or workflow matters in practice.
- Add grounded examples, diagnostic checks, edge cases, and next actions that demonstrate subject command.
- Use a consistent point of view. A strong editorial voice may say "we compared," "we reviewed," or "our analysis" when that work was actually performed.
- Reserve "we tested," "we used," "we tried," "we observed," and sensory judgments for documented firsthand records.
- When genuine author, customer, or expert experience exists, make it concrete: name the task, conditions, timeframe, relevant observation, and limits.
- Keep routine audit details in the claims ledger and quality report. Bring a limitation into the article only when it changes the recommendation or prevents a misleading inference.
- In methodology, describe the searches, inspections, comparisons, tests, and criteria actually used. Do not list absent test sets, unavailable inputs, hypothetical benchmark requirements, or process defenses such as "it does not rank measured realism" and "the publisher's product appears first because...."
- Keep list order reader-centered. Explain the organizing principle, such as input method, workflow, use case, or decision stage, rather than defending placement through publisher status.

Avoid credibility-subtracting meta-language such as:

- "This is not a hands-on comparison."
- "We did not run a controlled output test."
- "We do not present any tool as the universal winner."
- "We found no official basis for claiming..."

Replace those constructions with a useful positive judgment. For example, write "Example Tool is the strongest fit here for prompt-led browser exploration; use the repeatability check below to judge output consistency" instead of listing tests the author did not perform.

Do not hide material commercial relationships. Express them briefly and naturally in the byline, author note, methodology, or relevant product context. When a verified author or team is supplied, identify it directly. When only the publisher is verified, use a neutral pattern such as "In this [publisher] guide, every option is assessed against the same workflow criteria." Do not invent an editorial team, use a bold `Disclosure:` label by default, or follow the relationship line with multiple non-testing caveats. Follow any platform, legal, affiliate, or sponsorship wording the user is required to publish.

## Draft for direct comprehension

Lead important sections with the answer, recommendation, definition, or action. Follow with evidence, context, limits, and examples. Do not make readers traverse a generic setup to find the point.

Use short paragraphs by default, usually one idea per paragraph. Vary length when an explanation needs continuity. Avoid a mechanical rule such as exactly two sentences per paragraph.

Apply these writing rules:

- Use a clear H1 and logical H2/H3 hierarchy
- Make headings descriptive enough to stand alone
- Prefer concrete nouns and active verbs
- Define unfamiliar terms at first use
- Keep subjects and references explicit when ambiguity is possible
- Use lists for parallel items, steps for sequences, and tables for comparable fields
- Introduce every table or list and explain the takeaway when it is not obvious
- Keep qualifications close to the claim they limit
- State prerequisites before instructions and decision criteria before recommendations
- Preserve useful nuance without burying the direct answer
- Use examples that match the target audience and locale
- Remove generic hooks, throat-clearing, repeated conclusions, and empty transitions
- Avoid unsupported superlatives, artificial urgency, and exaggerated pain

Use PAS or PAES (Problem, Agitate, Expertise or Experience, Solution) only when the approved outline calls for it and it improves comprehension. Keep the problem and impact factual and proportionate, and use only real expertise or experience. These frameworks are optional and should not delay a direct answer.

For an approved PAES introduction:

1. **Problem:** Name the reader's concrete decision or failure mode in the first sentence or two.
2. **Agitate:** Show the practical cost of getting it wrong without manufacturing fear, urgency, regret, or unsupported outcomes.
3. **Expertise or Experience:** Earn trust with a traceable method, professional judgment, documented use session, test, interview, or supplied record. Prefer concrete criteria and observations to vague authority claims.
4. **Solution:** Answer the query, state the decision path, and transition naturally to the comparison, steps, or recommendation.

Keep the four beats compact and unlabeled in the published article. The introduction should still work as a direct answer when read without the rest of the page.

## Use keywords naturally

Write to cover the topic and intent, not to hit a keyword-density score.

- Use the primary query in the H1, opening, a relevant heading, or body only where it reads naturally.
- Use close variants, entities, attributes, and related questions when they add meaning.
- Do not set or report a fixed keyword-density target.
- Do not force the primary keyword or a variant into every section.
- Do not repeat a phrase merely because competitors use it frequently.
- Do not sacrifice grammar, local idiom, factual precision, or readability for exact-match placement.
- Remove keyword repetition when pronouns, shorter references, or specific entity names are clearer.

Treat SERP vocabulary as evidence of reader language and topic coverage, not as a list of required strings. A section can fully satisfy intent without containing the seed keyword.

## Apply the punctuation policy

Set `em_dash: disallow` as the default editorial configuration. Do not use the em dash character in drafted copy, headings, metadata, captions, or alt text under that default. Rewrite with a period, comma, colon, semicolon, or parentheses according to meaning.

Allow em dashes only when the user or project style guide explicitly sets `em_dash: allow`. Apply the selected rule consistently, including during later revisions and metadata work. Do not replace em dashes with awkward double hyphens.

Follow any supplied style guide for quotation marks, serial commas, capitalization, numerals, dates, units, and regional spelling. If none exists, use one consistent convention appropriate to the target language and locale.

## Write each supported format honestly

### Explainer

Give the plain-language definition early. Explain mechanism, significance, example, limits, misconceptions, and practical implications in the order the reader needs them. Separate established facts from interpretations.

### How-to

Use ordered, executable steps with real prerequisites, inputs, interface labels, and validation checks. Do not invent missing steps. Cover likely failure states only when fixes are verified. End with the observable finished state, not a generic encouragement.

### Listicle

Make every item distinct and useful. Use a consistent internal pattern when it aids scanning, but avoid repetitive prose. Include a concrete use case, example, or caveat rather than padding each item to equal length.

### Roundup

State the selection and evaluation method before recommendations. Evaluate all entries against consistent criteria and identify material relationships briefly and naturally, preferably in the byline, author note, or methodology. Describe work performed, not work omitted. Do not pair a relationship statement with missing-test details, hypothetical benchmark requirements, or an explanation that the publisher's product appears first. Use a reader-centered ordering principle and category or use-case recommendations when the evidence supports them. Do not imply every product received hands-on testing.

### Single Review

Lead with the verdict and intended user, then establish the evidence basis concisely in the methodology or first relevant claim. Distinguish observed behavior from documented features and third-party reports. Balance strengths with material limitations, identify good-fit and poor-fit users, and keep the verdict proportional to evidence. Do not turn the opening into an audit disclaimer.

### Comparison

Compare the same version, plan, timeframe, and criteria where possible. Explain why a difference matters instead of only listing features. Use `not confirmed` or omit a row when evidence is unavailable. Recommend by use case, constraints, or priorities rather than declaring a winner by default.

### Alternatives

Tie each alternative to a real switching reason. Explain what the reader gains, loses, must migrate, and may need to relearn. Do not call a product an alternative when it solves a materially different job.

## Use citations and attribution

Cite material facts close to the claim. Prefer primary sources for product behavior, specifications, prices, policies, laws, standards, and original research. Use credible independent sources for context, critique, and corroboration.

- Preserve the exact source URL and a descriptive source label.
- Distinguish publication date, update date, event date, and access date.
- Attribute opinions and disputed claims explicitly.
- Link to the most specific supporting page, not a homepage or search result.
- Do not cite a source that does not support the full sentence.
- Do not copy distinctive phrasing, competitor structures, or long passages.
- Quote only when exact wording is necessary, keep it brief, and respect applicable quotation limits and licenses.
- Record contradictions instead of silently choosing the most convenient source.

When inline citations are unsuitable for the requested platform, use its native footnote, endnote, reference, or link convention. Do not invent a citation style that breaks publishing requirements.

## Execute the media plan

Update the preliminary plan as assets are confirmed. For each asset, record:

- Final placement and the question it helps answer
- Type, filename or real URL, dimensions or aspect ratio, and format
- Creator or owner, source, license, permission, and required attribution
- Whether it is evidence, a real product screenshot, data visualization, diagram, stock media, or a labeled illustration
- Alt text that communicates the image's purpose without keyword stuffing
- Caption and source note when context or attribution is needed
- Responsive, compression, and lazy-loading notes for web delivery when relevant

Use real screenshots only when captured from the actual product or supplied by an authorized source. Do not construct a product interface and present it as a screenshot. Do not alter evidence images in a way that changes their meaning. Redact sensitive data and obtain permission before showing private accounts, people, messages, or documents.

Use charts only with traceable data, labeled axes, units, timeframe, source, and an accessible text takeaway. Mark generated or illustrative visuals clearly and never cite them as proof.

## Run the editing passes

Edit in separate passes so stylistic polishing cannot hide an evidence problem.

### 1. Intent and structure

- Confirm that the article fulfills the approved promise and primary format.
- Put direct answers where readers expect them.
- Remove duplicate sections and move misplaced information.
- Confirm that FAQ, PAA, tables, and other optional modules remain justified.

### 2. Accuracy and evidence

- Check every material claim against the ledger and source.
- Recheck time-sensitive facts.
- Mark inferences and opinions clearly.
- Remove unsupported first-person experience, quotes, examples, and comparisons.
- Resolve placeholders and `needs confirmation` items before delivery.

### 3. Information gain

- Confirm that original tests, data, interviews, or examples are documented.
- Make the useful difference from existing coverage explicit without boasting.
- Remove sections that merely paraphrase competitors.
- State evidence limitations where they affect the conclusion.

### 4. Clarity and style

- Shorten long openings and paragraphs where ideas can be separated.
- Replace vague claims with concrete language.
- Remove filler, repetition, cliches, and promotional drift.
- Remove defensive meta-language, repeated non-testing caveats, and QA narration from public copy.
- Confirm that the article expresses a professional point of view through specific judgments, reasons, examples, tradeoffs, and failure signals.
- Trace every experiential first-person verb to a real source, use session, test, interview, or other reviewable record.
- Check terminology, regional spelling, tone, and configured punctuation.
- Read headings and opening sentences as a standalone answer path.

### 5. Format integrity

- Verify ordered steps, consistent list logic, review disclosures, roundup methodology, comparison criteria, and alternative fit as applicable.
- Check tables row by row for like-for-like facts and unknown values.
- Keep conclusions proportional to the evidence.

### 6. Media and accessibility

- Confirm that every asset has a real purpose, rights status, and accurate label.
- Check alt text, captions, table headers, link text, and text equivalents.
- Remove broken, decorative, redundant, or misleading media.

### 7. Final language QA

- Check grammar, punctuation, names, numbers, units, dates, and link destinations.
- Search for em dashes when the default prohibition is active.
- Search for unresolved tokens, invented URLs, draft notes, and duplicated metadata.
- Confirm that no paragraph exists only to add keywords or word count.

Pass the edited draft to the SEO, structured-data, delivery, and final-approval checks. Do not label it approved or publish-ready before the required final approval.
