# SEO Metadata Rules

Use these rules to audit and draft Title and Meta Description candidates. Separate Google-backed quality guidance from practical editorial heuristics.

## Rule priority

Apply guidance in this order:

1. Page truth and verified facts.
2. Current Google Search Central guidance.
3. Intent and patterns observed in the live target-language SERP, refined by an explicit region when supplied.
4. Practical title and description copywriting heuristics.

Never let a length target, competitor pattern, or persuasive phrase override accuracy.

## Know what is being written

- The HTML `<title>` is the page owner's title preference. It is not the same as the H1 or Google's displayed title link.
- Google generates title links automatically from several page and link signals and may rewrite the supplied `<title>`.
- A meta description is a page-level summary supplied by the site owner. A Google snippet is query-dependent and may come from visible page content instead.
- Never promise that Google will display a proposed Title or Meta Description verbatim.

## Google-backed quality guidance

Apply these as strong checks:

- Give every page an accurate, descriptive, concise, and page-specific `<title>`.
- Keep the Title consistent with the page's main visual heading, H1, language, script, and actual content.
- Avoid vague labels, repeated boilerplate, keyword stuffing, stale dates, half-empty templates, and claims the page cannot support.
- Keep branding concise. Place it where it reads naturally; do not repeat the site name unnecessarily.
- Write a unique, accurate, relevant, human-readable Meta Description that summarizes the specific page and gives a truthful reason to click.
- Avoid lists of keywords, generic site-wide descriptions, duplicated descriptions, and copy that misrepresents the page.
- Use terms people search for in prominent text when they fit naturally. Do not require exact-match repetition.
- Report a keyword-to-page mismatch instead of forcing the keyword into irrelevant copy.
- Match the page's primary language and writing system. Do not apply English capitalization conventions to every locale.

Google does not publish fixed character limits for either element. Search results may truncate text according to device width, layout, and query.

## Editorial length targets

Use these as drafting targets, not Google compliance rules:

- Aim for 50–60 Latin-script characters for a Title.
- Aim for 120–158 Latin-script characters for a Meta Description.
- Treat full-width CJK characters as roughly two Latin display units. A practical target is often about 25–30 full-width characters for a Title and 60–79 for a Meta Description.
- Center the Title on one primary keyword and, when natural, one or two relevant secondary terms.
- Put the primary keyword early when doing so improves clarity.
- Do not append a brand or site-name suffix to the Title unless the brand is exceptionally well known in the target language audience and its recognition materially improves clarity, trust, or navigational intent. If the exception is uncertain, omit the suffix.
- Prefer one natural occurrence of the primary keyword in the Meta Description. When that would read awkwardly, use a semantically close secondary keyword with the same intent instead.
- Match the dominant SERP intent and useful result format, such as how-to, listicle, review, comparison, tool, or product page.
- For genuine listicles, consider a meaningful number. For guides, consider useful format signals such as a template, checklist, examples, or step-by-step coverage.
- Use benefit-led wording and conversational descriptions when appropriate.
- Front-load the most valuable page-specific information.
- Use dates, brackets, urgency, free offers, discounts, shipping claims, superlatives, or speed claims only when the page verifies them.
- Use SERP and ad language as inspiration for vocabulary and benefits; never copy another result.

English Title Case is an editorial option, not a Google requirement. Follow the locale and the brand's established style.

## Truthfulness guardrails

Never invent or exaggerate features, integrations, formats, platform support, availability, pricing, discounts, trial terms, speed, security, privacy, compliance, rankings, ratings, customer counts, or results. Treat terms such as `official`, `#1`, `best`, `free`, `instant`, `unlimited`, `no signup`, `new`, `updated`, and `latest` as claims that require evidence from the page.

Competitor pages and ads are copy research, not evidence for claims about the target page.

## Internal audit checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

1. The primary keyword, detected or supplied language, search environment or explicit region, and dominant intent are established.
2. The Title and Meta Description accurately describe the target page.
3. The Title aligns with the H1 or main visual title without merely duplicating it by default.
4. Each pair is unique within the current item, and metadata is not duplicated across a supplied batch.
5. No Title ends with a brand or site name unless current evidence supports the strict exceptional-brand rule.
6. Each Meta Description contains the primary keyword or a semantically close secondary keyword unless inclusion would be inaccurate or unnatural.
7. Keywords read naturally and are not stacked or repeated mechanically.
8. Dates, numbers, benefits, features, prices, offers, and calls to action are supported by evidence.
9. The wording fits the locale and writing system.
10. Each pair approaches the editorial display-width targets without sacrificing clarity.
11. Pair 1 is the strongest balance of intent match, accuracy, clarity, differentiation, and likely click appeal.
12. Exactly five pairs are ready, with no audit or research commentary intended for the successful response.
13. Any SERP-derived supporting vocabulary used in a Title comes from the Title frequency table, and any SERP-derived supporting vocabulary used in a Meta Description comes from the snippet frequency table.
14. Recurring SERP terms remain natural, page-supported, and selectively distributed instead of being forced into every pair.
